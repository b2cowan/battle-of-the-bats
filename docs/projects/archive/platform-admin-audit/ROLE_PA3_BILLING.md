# PA3 — Billing Specialist Operator Report
> Walked: 2026-06-13 | Method: code-walk (Stage B) | Status: draft | Verify: risk-targeted (High/bug findings)

---

## The operator at a glance

The billing specialist owns the financial health of the customer base. Their shift starts in the Retention Queue — scanning for accounts nearing data purge — and moves through org-level cancellations, override and comp grants, bulk exceptions, and account-access triage. This is the role with the most consequential write surface in the console after super admin: a billing specialist can cancel subscriptions, apply status overrides, extend retention deadlines, bulk-grant comp periods, and reset customer passwords. Their nav is notably wider than the support rep's but narrower than the product operator's: no Observability/Feedback, no Email Templates, no Early Access.

The good news is that the billing specialist's primary job — Retention, Org Billing & Access, Overrides, Bulk Operations — works end-to-end without silent failures. Where the support rep hit 403s on every Retention action, billing correctly holds `manage_billing`, so both `+30 days` and `Process expiry` succeed. Where support saw disabled form fields in the Billing & Access tab, billing sees fully enabled forms backed by correctly gated API routes. The core workflow is functional.

The experience degrades in three places. First, the Billing & Access tab renders a view-only warning banner for non-billing roles, but the cancel-subscription section is fully hidden when `canManageBilling=false` — there is no "billing does this, not you" affordance for support reps who get tab access and cannot understand why cancel is absent. Second, the Entitlements tab requires `manage_product`, which billing does not hold, so billing specialists can see the Module Overrides section but cannot interact with it — and the copy says "Requires product access" without telling them who to ask or how to escalate. Third, the Change Requests list is view-only for billing, but none of the action buttons (Approve, Apply, Reject) visually disable or explain why — they appear clickable until they fail, reintroducing the same "silent dead-end" pattern that plagued PA2 on Retention.

Day-one orientation for billing is materially stronger than for support: the Help Hub has good Billing SOP content, the Retention queue and Billing & Access tab are self-explanatory, and the Bulk Operations surface has a well-structured three-step flow. But several friction points, a silent dead-end on Change Requests, and no Stripe-side link from within the console limit what a first-shift employee can accomplish alone.

## Day-one verdict

**Borderline — no for the full billing job, yes for the core billing job.**

A brand-new billing hire can orient quickly and complete the most common billing tasks on shift one without tribal knowledge: cancel a subscription (SOP present, preflight modal is clear), extend a retention deadline (queue is legible, form is self-explanatory), add a billing override (the Add Override form is well-structured with a type selector and required reason field), run a bulk status override or comp period grant (Bulk Operations is the best-designed surface in the role). The Audit Log reinforces every action.

The new hire hits trouble in three areas. First, the Change Requests page appears to offer action buttons (see PAB-004) that silently fail — a replication of the support seam bug for a different role. Second, expired overrides require pro-active hunting across every org; there is no queue or filter that surfaces them the way the Retention Queue surfaces at-risk retention records (PAB-002). Third, when a billing task requires product access — module toggles in Entitlements, running a `module_addon_enablement` bulk op — the handoff path is "requires product access" with no escalation route. A new hire cannot identify who the product operator is or how to contact them from within the console.

The Help Hub material is the strongest asset: the Billing SOP, Cancellation SOP, Retention SOP, and Bulk Operations SOP are all present and well-written. The gap is navigation — there is no "Billing Specialist" role-path card to surface them at login.

## Top 5 moves

1. **Fix Change Requests action-button silent failure for billing (PAB-004 — High).** Audit every write-action button in `ChangeRequestsClient.tsx` to confirm `canManageProduct` disables/hides them. Add a "Review-only — requires product access" note when `canManageProduct=false`. This eliminates the most trust-damaging failure mode (click → silent 403) for the billing role.

2. **Add expired-override filter to the Orgs list (PAB-002 — High).** A billing specialist's shift should start with a queryable set of expired overrides, not a count on the overview dashboard that links to an unfiltered org list. An attention filter matching the existing pattern (e.g. "Expired overrides") gives billing a zero-extra-navigation path to their daily housekeeping backlog.

3. **Add Cancel Subscription stub when `canManageBilling=false` (PAB-003 — High).** Currently the section vanishes; support reps and read-only roles land on Billing & Access and cannot tell that Cancel exists but requires a different role. A stub with "Cancellation requires billing access — contact the billing team" closes the discovery gap and prevents "is this broken?" confusion. Matches the existing Delete Organization stub pattern.

4. **Make org-detail tab URL-addressable (PAB-008 — Med).** Adding `?tab=billing` as a query param removes the most repeated friction point in the billing workflow (Retention → org → must click to Billing & Access every time). Enables deep-linking from Retention rows, from the overview action queue, and from colleague references.

5. **Add a Stripe dashboard deep-link to the org detail page (PAB-009 — Med).** The Help SOP references "Open Stripe link on the org detail page" but no such link exists. A billing specialist handling a failed Stripe cancellation must copy the subscription ID and navigate to Stripe manually. A direct link to `dashboard.stripe.com/subscriptions/{id}` eliminates that context-switch entirely.

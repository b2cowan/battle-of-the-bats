# J3 — House-League Administrator ("Maria") Journey Report

> Walked: 2026-06-11 | Method: code-walk (12-agent workflow, risk-targeted adversarial verify) + live browser (28 screenshots — re-captured post harness fix, 3 design-lens passes) + auditor live verification | Tree state: feat/free-tier-coaches @ uncommitted working tree | Status: draft | Findings routed: 2026-06-11 (FREE_TIER_STRATEGY §16 extension; remainder backlog per report)

**Context:** League is `early_access` by locked design — there is deliberately no self-serve checkout. Per the audit plan's framing, "no path to buy" findings are *missing-feature* documentation of the as-is reality (routed to the owning plan), not bugs. The expectation gap is applied throughout: Maria is a volunteer registrar/scheduler/treasurer running ~200 kids, $150 e-transfer fees, a spreadsheet, Gmail, and a Facebook group — the product's competition is not another SaaS, it's her spreadsheet.

## The journey at a glance

The surprise of this journey is that the admin product Maria cannot yet buy is genuinely good. The registrations surface on desktop is the strongest screen any audited persona has been handed: it defaults to Pending Review, its tab counts matched the staged data exactly, approve/waitlist/decline are inline and colour-coded, waitlist auto-promotion renumbers positions and emails the promoted family, and the CSV export carries DOB and guardian contact. The season detail page works as real mission control — capacity bars with waitlist annotation, registration window and fee, per-division quick links including a working money ledger — and standings compute correctly with a plain-words points-and-tiebreaker legend a volunteer can forward to a skeptical coach. **The verdict: a genuinely good admin core, wrapped in a broken shell — an express-interest-only front door on one side, and a public face that is an unbranded stub telling parents the wrong thing on the other.**

The journey's sharpest find never appears on a marketing page: **non-tournament admin APIs resolve org context from the caller's *first membership row*** (J3-012, auditor-contributed, fix-now). The first screenshot capture of this walk hit it live — a multi-org owner opening `/dev-league-org/admin/house-league` was silently resolved to *dev-test-org*, producing a bare "ERROR / Forbidden / CLOSE" modal and a dead-end "Season not found." page. That was real product behaviour, not a harness bug. 78 route files / 121 call sites — effectively all of house-league, accounting, rep-teams, members, org-settings, and public-site APIs — never pass the org slug that is sitting right there in the page URL, so for modules the arbitrary first org *is* entitled to, reads **and writes** land in whichever org sorts first. A club president who owns two orgs could post ledger entries into the wrong org's books. Single-org users never see it, which is why it has survived; J4's persona is multi-org by definition and walks straight into it.

Arrival is where trust erodes first. Maria's very first authenticated screen shows live Public Site and House League tiles — then ghosted, locked duplicates of those same modules under "Coming soon — League & Club plans" (J3-014): the hub tells a paying League customer her $89/mo plan isn't real yet. Nothing ever routes her into the league onboarding wizard; the only pointer is a rocket banner permanently mislabelled "First tournament setup available" (J3-015). The wizard itself is calm and well-defaulted, but it plants a season-long trap — registration open/close datetimes that *control nothing* and are stored with a hardcoded `Z` so they render hours off (J3-018) — and then drops her back on the generic tile grid instead of her new season (J3-023). Her Tuesday-night goal is achievable in ~15 minutes right up to its final step: after clicking Open Registration, **the public registration URL appears nowhere in the entire admin house-league tree** (J3-027) — she cannot paste the one link her whole evening was for, and the lifecycle button she just pressed is single-click, forward-only, and irreversible (J3-028).

In-season, the product wins exactly where it's finished and loses exactly where it isn't. Intake, waitlist mechanics, the fee ledger's Expected/Collected/Outstanding tiles, and live standings all beat the spreadsheet. But the schedule generator produces pairings, not a schedule — every game in a round stamped with one time and one free-text location, "Rounds per week: 2" double-booking both rounds onto the *same night* (J3-045, J3-046) — and every game and practice time is stored timezone-naive, so on UTC prod hardware Maria's 6:00 PM renders as 2:00 PM to every parent (J3-047, fix-now before any League org goes live). Parent-submitted medical and carpool notes are invisible everywhere except a CSV export (J3-030); review night is one blocking success modal per child, two hundred times (J3-031); the only way to record a family quitting is a "Decline" that emails them "your registration was not approved" (J3-032); and the draft is one step short of draft night (no snake order, no visible rosters, Division A's draft locks out Division B).

The communications layer is the gap between promise and product. The platform's own upsell email sells House League as "registration, draft, schedule, standings, parent notifications — *no manual emails*"; the reality is zero lifecycle email of any kind (J3-062), a Notifications page whose Waitlist/Pending audiences silently email nobody while showing a green "Email sent — 0 delivered" success (J3-058), broadcast counts that report provider failures as delivered (J3-059), no reply path on any guardian email — declined families are told to contact the platform's own Gmail (J3-060) — and no coach channel at all: "coach" is a display-only text label, so rosters and rainouts go out from Maria's personal inbox, exactly as before (J3-061). On rainout night, the product loses to Facebook.

The public face is where a volunteer's reputation lives, and it is the journey's most dangerous surface. The one Facebook link Maria posts breaks the day next year's registration opens — the active season, with all its schedules and standings, vanishes from public navigation entirely (J3-067). The public schedule never says *where* games are played (J3-071), silently drops cancelled games, and shows postponed games at their original time as if they're on (J3-070) — parents drive to dark diamonds. The status-lookup page is the single worst find of the audit so far: an unauthenticated oracle that returns any child's full name, age division, and status to anyone who types a guardian's email — even on private orgs (J3-069, **Blocker**, fix-now). And all of it is wrapped in an unbranded, lime-on-black stub that the design lens scored an outright fail next to the broadcast-grade tournament public stack the same org gets in July (J3-074). The two fixes that matter most: thread org context through every admin API (the platform fix), and ship the public-trust package — oracle, gating, schedule honesty, timezone — before League is promoted to anyone (the journey fix).

## Top 5 moves

2. **Make the public league surfaces safe to share before anyone shares them** (J3-069 Blocker + J3-068, fix-now · J3-047 fix-now · J3-070, J3-071, J3-067). The package: gate the status oracle behind email + reference code, apply the index's org-gating to all five sub-pages and the register API, store real timezones so 6:00 PM means 6:00 PM in prod, show cancellations/postponements truthfully, render the diamond on every game row, and keep the active season navigable when next year's registration opens. Every item here is a parent driving to a dark diamond or a child's data leaking — the two ways a volunteer admin loses her league's trust permanently.
3. **Repair the communications spine** (J3-058, J3-059, J3-060, J3-062, J3-072; J3-061 as the FTS-routed sibling). Today the composer tells Maria "Email sent — 0 delivered" in green, counts provider failures as sent, replies vanish into the platform's Gmail, and a rainout notifies no one — while the upsell email promises "parent notifications — no manual emails." Fix the status-value mismatch and honest counts first (small, verified, shippable), then reply-to + league identity, then the notify-affected-families loop on game changes and payment instructions at approval. This is the layer that replaces Facebook+Gmail — the product's whole pitch.
4. **Make the generator produce a schedule, not pairings** (J3-045, J3-046, J3-048, J3-049). Day-of-week pickers instead of the round-stacking "rounds per week" math, venue/time slots the pairings deal into (reuse org Venues), an exists-already guard on regenerate, and always-visible score inputs. Scheduling around two shared diamonds is the core league-admin job and the one place the walk concluded "the spreadsheet is honestly faster."
5. **Win the weeknight at 200-kid scale** (J3-031, J3-030, J3-032, J3-034, J3-027, J3-028). Bulk approve with toasts instead of 200 blocking modals, a registration drawer that finally shows the medical/carpool notes parents already submitted, a real Withdraw action that stops emailing quitting families "not approved," an unpaid filter with a dollar rollup, the public link + share moment the instant registration opens, and confirmations on the one-way lifecycle. This cluster is the difference between "better than my spreadsheet" and "worse, but prettier."

## 1. Mission Statement and Elevator Pitch

### Mission Statement (single sentence)

> FieldLogicHQ exists so the people who run community sport can spend more of their time watching it.

**Why this formulation:**  
It answers "what is FieldLogicHQ?" for someone with no context — a board member, a potential investor, a sport body contact. It leads with purpose, not product. It doesn't duplicate the positioning statement ("operating platform for people who run community sport") — it gives the *why* behind the platform rather than describing the *what*. It's persona-agnostic: it works for a tournament director, a league president, a club exec, and a coach.

The line is not a headline — it's a mission. Resist the urge to use it as a homepage hero. It belongs on an About page, a pitch deck, or in a grant or partnership application.

---

### Elevator Pitch (3 sentences)

> FieldLogicHQ is an operating platform for volunteer-run sports organizations — from single tournaments to full club programs. Tournament brackets, house league seasons, rep team management, and coaching tools all live in one place, so an org president isn't stitching together five different apps just to run a season. We're in our founding year — Tournament Plus is free through the end of 2026 for organizations that get started now.

**Usage context:** Pitch deck, About page, press kit, partnership outreach, founding season email signature or cold outreach opener. The third sentence is founding-season-specific — retire it after December 31, 2026.

**Variation — without founding season offer (use after Dec 31, 2026):**

> FieldLogicHQ is an operating platform for volunteer-run sports organizations — from single tournaments to full club programs. Tournament brackets, house league seasons, rep team management, and coaching tools all live in one place, so an org president isn't stitching together five different apps just to run a season. It's built for the people running leagues on evenings and weekends — for the kids, not a salary.

Use for `/about` when that page is created. Not a headline — a narrative opener.

## 2. User Acquisition Strategy

### Primary Audience Reality Check

Volunteer sports org administrators are not reading SaaS newsletters. They are:
- In Facebook groups and community pages for their sport and region
- Responding to emails from their provincial sport bodies
- Googling a specific problem when they're already frustrated ("how to make a tournament bracket", "youth hockey tournament software")
- Getting recommendations from the person they replaced when they took over the role
- Coaching at the same tournaments as every other org in their region

Acquisition strategy must start with where they actually are — not where it would be convenient to reach them.

---

**Phase 1 — Founding Season (May–December 2026, 0→50 Tournament Plus orgs)**

| Priority | Channel | Why | Who |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Targeted cold outreach | Highest conversion potential; direct-to-decision-maker; founding season offer reduces friction to near zero | Tournament directors, league presidents |
| 2 | SEO — problem-aware queries | Passive inbound; works on zero budget; tournament organizers google this problem exactly | Tournament organizers, coaches |
| 3 | Facebook community engagement | Authentic presence in groups where the audience already is; not broadcast — participation | All four segments |
| 4 | Post-tournament coach outreach (active program) | Coaches who've touched the tournament UI are warm contacts; highest conversion to org intro | Individual coaches → org introduction |
| 5 | Word of mouth / referral | Every org using the platform knows other orgs; the founding cohort becomes the referral network | Tournament organizers |

**Phase 2 — Post-Founding Season (2027+, 50→500)**

| Priority | Channel | Why |

| 4 | Email lifecycle and nurture | Express interest list for League/Club converts to paid when those tiers open |

### Segment-by-Segment Acquisition Motion

#### Segment 1 — Tournament Organizers (Active now)

**Entry point:** Google search ("tournament scheduling", "sports bracket generator", "tournament management software") or cold outreach or word of mouth from another director.

**Funnel:**
1. Lands on `/for-tournament-organizers` or homepage
2. Self-identifies with persona card; clicks through
3. Signs up free — no credit card, no friction
4. Runs first tournament (the platform has to earn this; onboarding must be fast)
5. Founding Season offer surfaces during onboarding or at tournament wrap
6. Converts to Tournament Plus (free through Dec 31)
7. Post-tournament email: "Your tournament's wrapped. Your history is here. Here's what else your org could run from FieldLogicHQ."
8. Upsell path: becomes a League or Club prospect if they're also a league/club operator

**Cold outreach targeting:**  
Identify tournament directors via: provincial sport association event calendars (publicly listed tournaments), Facebook event listings (youth sports tournaments), association websites with director contact info, and sport-specific registration platforms that list events publicly. Reach out by email only; keep it 3 sentences or less; reference the specific tournament or sport. The founding season offer is the hook — make it specific to their current problem, not a generic pitch.

**Cold outreach template (NOT public-facing copy; internal use only):**

> Subject: Running a tournament this spring/summer?
>
> Hi [Name] — I saw your [league/association] is running [Tournament Name] in [month]. FieldLogicHQ handles tournament registration, scheduling, brackets, and live scores in one place — and it's free to start, no credit card.
>
> We're in our founding year and offering Tournament Plus free through the end of 2026. Happy to show you around if it's useful timing. [Link to /for-tournament-organizers]

Personalization requirement: Always reference the specific sport, the specific tournament or org, and send before the event (not after).

---

#### Segment 2 — House League Administrators (Not yet purchasable)

**Current state:** League plan is not yet open. Express interest captures email.

**Acquisition motion:**
1. Tournament organizer who's also a league admin discovers the platform through the tournament funnel
2. Homepage and `/for-leagues` provide clear path to express interest
3. Email list is nurtured with product updates (not volume; only meaningful sends)
4. When League opens: warm list converts to paid

**Content priority:** One well-ranked how-to article targeting "how to run a house league registration" or "youth hockey league scheduling" would feed this funnel before the plan is purchasable.

**Do not:** Push hard on this segment pre-launch. Over-communication to a not-yet-available product builds resentment, not anticipation.

---

#### Segment 3 — Club Executives (Not yet purchasable)

**Acquisition motion:**
1. Almost always enters through the tournament funnel — club execs often also run or oversee tournaments
2. Sees "Club — coming soon" on homepage and in `/for-clubs`
3. Express interest captures email; nurture with relevant club-focused updates
4. Coach advocacy program is a secondary entry point: coach who's been using Coaches Portal asks org president to look at Club
5. When Club opens: warm list converts; coaches already in the org make the internal sale easier

**Cold outreach (Phase 2 only):** Club execs are harder to reach cold and need more trust before converting. In Phase 1, the tournament funnel and coach-as-advocate are the right entry points. Direct cold outreach to club presidents should wait until there are 10+ reference orgs and a case study or two.

---

#### Segment 4 — Individual Head Coaches (Not yet purchasable)

**Acquisition motion:**
1. **Primary entry point:** Post-tournament email to coaches who participated as team contacts in a tournament run on FieldLogicHQ. This is the highest-intent touchpoint.
2. Coach sees the platform working during tournament; curiosity is already present
3. Email introduces the Coaches Portal and the bridge message
4. Coach signs up for early access or express interest list
5. Active coach program: follow up personally; offer first access; ask for org introduction

**Active coach program mechanics:**
- After each tournament wraps, pull the list of team contacts (coaches/managers) from the platform
- Send a personal-feeling (not broadcast) email referencing their specific tournament
- Offer a Coaches Portal preview or first-access slot when it opens
- Follow-up email asks: "Is your organization considering anything like this for their broader program? We'd love to connect with whoever handles that."
- This is the highest-leverage organic channel for Club acquisition

**Bridge message (always include in coach-facing outreach):**
> "If your organization isn't on FieldLogicHQ yet, we still have a product for you. When they join, your workspace carries over automatically — no starting over."

## 3. Go-to-Market Sequencing

### Milestone Definition

**Founding season goal: 50 organizations on Tournament Plus by December 31, 2026.**

Tournament Plus is free through founding season. The conversion to paid (currently $39/month) happens January 1, 2027 for orgs that signed up under the founding offer. These become the first paying customer cohort. The 50-org target is not just a usage milestone — it's the founding customer cohort that proves willingness to pay when billing goes live.

**Why 50?**  
50 orgs across different sports and regions generates enough variety to:
- Identify platform edge cases before billing goes live
- Build a meaningful reference base for Phase 2 acquisition
- Create enough word-of-mouth density to generate passive inbound
- Validate pricing before League and Club open for purchase

---

### 0 → 50 Plan (Founding Season: May–December 2026)

**Outreach ownership:** Founder-led. Single-person operation. 10–15 targeted, personalized emails per week is the sustainable rate alongside active product development. At that pace with a realistic 20% response rate and 30% conversion, outreach alone generates 2–4 new orgs per month — sufficient to hit 50 by November without sacrificing product quality.

**The rule before outreach begins:** Don't start outreach until the full tournament flow — signup → setup → registration → schedule → score entry → wrap — is smooth enough to hand to a stranger. An org that hits a confusing step and abandons tells their peers. That's worse than no acquisition. The product readiness gate comes first.

---

**Phase 1 — Product ready (May–June 2026): No outreach yet**
- [ ] Finish tournament admin design review
- [ ] Complete Stripe Phase G (go-live — required before founding season conversions are meaningful)
- [ ] Fix Phase 1 audit copy issues: 14-day trial language, "free plan" wording (see brand audit)
- [ ] Verify founding season offer is accurate and consistent across all public surfaces
- [ ] Publish first 2 guides (see Section 5) — SEO setup takes time to index; earlier is better

**Why hold outreach until July:** The founding season offer requires billing to be live and credible. Stripe Phase G needs to be complete so that when an org asks "what happens after December?" there's a real answer. Running outreach before that creates false expectations.

- [ ] Send first outreach batch: 10–15 personalized emails to fall tournament directors

**How to find contacts:**

- Club websites that list their annual events and tournament directors

**Personalization minimum:** Reference the specific sport, the specific tournament or org by name, and send 4–6 weeks before their event (when they're actively planning, not the week before). Generic outreach to sports orgs is ignored. "I saw the [Tournament Name] is scheduled for [month] — FieldLogicHQ handles registration and brackets for events like yours, and it's free to start" gets opened.

- [ ] Second outreach batch: 30–50 emails/week — fall tournament season is the biggest acquisition window of the year for hockey, soccer, basketball

- [ ] Target: 30 orgs on Tournament Plus by end of October
- [ ] Activate coach advocacy program at scale: personal outreach to coaches from all completed tournaments
- [ ] Organic word of mouth begins: coaches from competing orgs encounter the platform through tournaments; org presidents who were at the same tournament ask who ran the schedule

- [ ] Final push to hit 50 before Dec 31

### 50 → 500 Plan (2027+)

The founding cohort of 50 orgs is the foundation for everything in Phase 2. The strategy shifts from founder-driven outreach to system-driven growth.

**The three Phase 2 growth levers:**

**Lever 1: Organic word of mouth**  
50 orgs run their seasons. Each org has coaches, parents, and administrators who see the product. Each tournament a FieldLogicHQ org runs exposes coaches from *other* orgs to the platform — they receive score and standings updates, interact with the bracket, and encounter the brand directly. Tournament directors and org presidents in the same sport community talk to each other constantly. No formal referral program is needed; the product quality and the existing network density are the referral mechanism. The job in Phase 2 is to make sure the product keeps earning those conversations.

**Lever 2: Tier expansion**  
When League and Club open for purchase, the entire express interest list — plus the tournament funnel — becomes a conversion pool. Many tournament organizers on Tournament Plus are also league or club operators. The conversion email to existing orgs ("You've run your tournament here. Your house league season can live here too.") is the highest-ROI send in Phase 2.

**Lever 3: Partnership distribution**  
Provincial and national sport body partnerships unlock entire directories of orgs at once. See Section 2 (partnership channel) and below.

---

### Partnership Strategy (Provincial and National Sport Bodies)

**Why this is a 50→500 lever, not a 0→50 lever:**  
Sport body partnerships require trust, demonstrated platform stability, and usually a reference base ("tell us who's using it"). Starting partnership conversations in the final month of founding season (October–December 2026) is appropriate — these conversations take 3–6 months to progress. Results land in early-to-mid 2027.

**Target body types:**  
1. **Provincial sport associations** (e.g., Ontario Soccer, Hockey Manitoba, Baseball BC) — they run sanctioned tournaments and have directories of affiliated clubs and leagues. A partnership means the association recommends or lists FieldLogicHQ as a preferred tournament management tool for sanctioned events.
2. **Regional tournament series organizers** — multi-event series (provincial championships, invitational circuits) that involve many clubs. One relationship covers multiple orgs.
3. **National federation affiliates** (Hockey Canada, Baseball Canada, Canada Soccer affiliates) — higher leverage, longer timeline. Phase 2 or 3 only.

**What a partnership looks like (practical):**  
- Listed as recommended tool in their annual tournament organizer guide or newsletter
- Mentioned in their onboarding materials for new member clubs
- Co-branded communication to member orgs ("Association-recommended — try FieldLogicHQ free")
- Not a formal contract in Phase 2 — a relationship and a recommendation is sufficient initially

**Partnership pitch positioning:**  
> "FieldLogicHQ handles the tournament administration work that volunteers in your affiliated orgs are currently doing manually. We're not a scoring sponsor — we're what replaces the Google Forms, the bracket spreadsheets, and the group texts. We offer CAD pricing and we're designed specifically for the volunteer-run context your members operate in."

**Outreach approach:**  
- Start with the sport body's event coordinator or development director (not the CEO)
- Reference a tournament or affiliated club already using the platform (requires founding cohort first)
- The ask is low-stakes: "Would you be willing to mention us in your next tournament organizer communication?"

## 4. Competitive Differentiation Framework

*This section is internal only. Public-facing copy never names competitors; it positions against the old way.*

### The Alternatives We're Actually Displacing

---

#### Segment 1 — Tournament Organizers

**What they're using today:**
- Google Forms or JotForm for team registration
- Google Sheets or Excel for brackets and scheduling
- Email and WhatsApp for score updates and announcements
- Sometimes: Challonge, BracketHQ, Tourney Machine for brackets only

**Why the current approach breaks down:**
- Schedule conflicts aren't visible until a coach calls on a Saturday morning
- Bracket tools don't handle registration, communication, or standings — they're one piece
- Starting from scratch every year; no institutional memory
- Score updates require a central person to maintain manually

**Why FieldLogicHQ wins:**

| Their alternative | Our answer |
|---|---|
| Google Forms + spreadsheets | End-to-end in one place; no manual data transfer between tools |
| Challonge/BracketHQ | Equal or better bracket tools, but registration and standings are native — not an afterthought |
| "We've always done it this way" | The first tournament is free and takes under an hour to set up; we just need them to try it |
| SportsEngine | Over-engineered and priced for large clubs; FieldLogicHQ is sized for a volunteer org running one tournament a year |

**Compounding advantage:** Every tournament creates an archive. Orgs return because their history is here. Challonge doesn't do that. This is the moat.

---

#### Segment 2 — House League Administrators

**What they're using today:**
- Registration via Google Forms, JotForm, or their provincial association's own system
- Team assignment and draft management in spreadsheets
- Schedule built manually in Excel, often by a volunteer who inherited the file from the previous year
- Standings maintained in a Google Sheet shared with the executive
- Parent communication from personal email inboxes

**Why the current approach breaks down:**
- Registration data isn't connected to scheduling or teams — manual re-entry
- Standings updates require a volunteer to log in after every game night and remember to do it
- When the registrar moves on, the knowledge moves with them
- No self-serve parent portal; all questions come back to the same volunteer inbox

**Why FieldLogicHQ wins:**

| Their alternative | Our answer |
|---|---|
| LeagueApps | Strong product but priced for paid-staff organizations; USD pricing; requires a different scale of org to justify |
| SportsEngine | Same as above; built for large clubs, not a 200-player rec league run by volunteers |
| TeamSnap | Team management, not org/season management; doesn't handle registration, drafts, or org-level standings |
| HockeyShift (Canada) | Hockey-specific; FieldLogicHQ is sport-agnostic and handles the full season arc |
| Spreadsheets | The org already knows this isn't working; they just need a path out |

**The CAD pricing advantage:** A meaningful differentiator for Canadian orgs. SportsEngine and LeagueApps are USD. A $89 CAD/month tool that does everything a $150 USD/month tool does wins on price alone — before we even account for the product differences.

---

#### Segment 3 — Club and Rep Team Organizations

**What they're using today:**
- Org admin uses a combination of spreadsheets, email, and possibly a team management app for some coaches
- Individual coaches manage their teams in TeamSnap, WhatsApp groups, or nothing
- Tryouts managed by email chain; no central intake
- Team finances tracked independently per coach (or not tracked at all)
- Org exec has no visibility without asking each coaching staff directly

**Why the current approach breaks down:**
- The org exec has no way to see roster status, team finances, or document compliance without asking each coach
- Coaches operate in different tools; there's no org-wide coherence
- When a coaching staff changes, the team's history disappears
- Registration, tryouts, and program years are all separate efforts, often redone from scratch each year

**Why FieldLogicHQ wins:**

| Their alternative | Our answer |
|---|---|
| TeamSnap per-team | Solves team management but gives the org exec nothing; no visibility, no coherence |
| SportsEngine org tools | Expensive, complex, built for paid-staff operations; not calibrated for a volunteer-run club |
| Separate tools stitched together | The problem is the stitching — every tool boundary is a place data gets lost or duplicated |
| "Our system works fine" | It works until a coaching staff turns over; we're the institutional memory solution |

**The differentiated offer:** No other platform at this price point gives the org exec visibility *and* gives coaches autonomy. That's the specific value proposition. Frame every Club conversation around: the exec gets what they need without managing the day-to-day; the coaches get tools without org bureaucracy.

---

#### Segment 4 — Individual Head Coaches

**What they're using today:**
- Roster: group text, team management app (TeamSnap), or a spreadsheet
- Lineups: notes app, whiteboard, verbal
- Team budget: personal tracking (or nothing formal)
- Documents: email chains, shared Google Drive folder that nobody can find
- Schedule: PDF sent at the start of the season, immediately outdated

**Why the current approach breaks down:**
- Nothing is connected; the roster in TeamSnap is different from the lineup in Notes is different from the budget in someone's head
- When the coach changes, everything resets
- Travel documents get lost; consent forms expire and nobody notices
- Parents and players have no self-serve access; everything goes through the coach's personal email

**Why FieldLogicHQ wins:**

| Their alternative | Our answer |
|---|---|
| TeamSnap | Consumer-grade; no lineup history, no budget tools, no document management |
| HomeTurf | Roster and communication focus; budget and documents are not central features |
| Nothing (most coaches) | Low bar; any organized system wins; the Coaches Portal just needs to be simple enough to use in a 5-minute onboarding |

**The bridge advantage:** When the coach's org eventually joins FieldLogicHQ, their workspace carries over. No competitor can offer this — it requires an integrated org-level platform. This is exclusive to us.

### Competitive Positioning Summary (for internal use)

We win on three things, across all four segments:

1. **End-to-end integration** — not a bracket tool, not a team app, not a scheduler. The season lives in one place, from first registration to final standings.
2. **Volunteer-operator calibration** — pricing, complexity, and support model are designed for people doing this on evenings and weekends, not for paid-staff operations at large clubs.
3. **Institutional memory** — every tournament archived, every season recorded, every roster preserved. When volunteers turn over, the platform doesn't.

The alternatives either do one thing well (Challonge for brackets, TeamSnap for team management) or are built for a much larger, more funded context (SportsEngine, LeagueApps). We're the only end-to-end platform priced and calibrated for the volunteer-run community sport context.

## 5. Content and Channel Strategy

### Two types of guide content — don't conflate them

There are two distinct content surfaces that both look like "guides" but serve completely different audiences and jobs:

| | **`/guides/` — Marketing guides** | **`/help/` — In-app help** |
|---|---|---|
| **Audience** | Someone who hasn't signed up yet — found us through Google | An existing user stuck on a specific task |
| **Job** | Show the problem is solvable here; earn the signup | Answer the specific question; keep them moving |
| **Tone** | Shows the pain, walks the full solution, introduces FieldLogicHQ naturally | Direct, procedural, assumes they're already in the product |
| **Format** | 1,200–2,000 words; full workflow; worked example; CTA at end | Short, task-focused; numbered steps; minimal prose |
| **Discoverability** | Google search — external acquisition | In-app nav, contextual help links |

These must not be the same page. A marketing guide that reads like help documentation loses the acquisition job. In-app help that reads like a marketing article is confusing when you're stuck on step 3.

The in-app help surface (and any frustrations with how it has worked so far) is a separate problem worth a dedicated session — likely about navigation architecture, content depth calibration, or the gap between what the help says and what the UI does. The guides strategy below is exclusively about external marketing content.

---

### No blog — a `/guides` section instead

The content program is **not a blog**. A blog implies a feed, a publication cadence, an author voice, and a reason for people to come back regularly. Your audience doesn't subscribe to sports management blogs. They search for a specific answer when they have a specific problem, find it, and leave.

What the strategy needs is a **`/guides` section** — a small library of practical, evergreen how-to articles. The distinction matters:

| Blog | Guides section |
|---|---|
| Feed-based, dated, ordered by publish date | Topic-organized, undated, organized by audience |
| Implies ongoing publication and editorial ownership | Written once, updated when needed |
| Readers subscribe and return | Readers arrive from Google, solve their problem, convert |
| Needs a moderator and contributor workflow | Needs an editor once per piece |

A dated blog post signals it might be outdated. An undated guide signals it's the definitive reference. "How to run a round-robin tournament" lives at `/guides/round-robin-tournament` — not `/blog/2026-06-round-robin`. The URL structure is a trust signal.

**No moderation or editorial pipeline is needed.** Each guide is a standalone asset. Write it, publish it, update it if the product changes. That's the entire content operation at this stage.

---

### Guide structure template

Each guide follows this structure. The FieldLogicHQ product appears naturally at the moments where a tool would be useful — not as the premise.

**Example: `/guides/round-robin-tournament`**

```
Title: How to Run a Round-Robin Tournament (Step-by-Step Guide)

Opening (1 short paragraph):
  You've got 16 teams, 3 fields, and a Saturday. Here's exactly how to build 
  the schedule, manage registration, and make sure coaches have what they need 
  before the first game.

Section 1: Pick your format (~200 words)
  What round-robin means, when it makes sense vs. elimination formats, typical 
  team counts. One table: teams × games per team × total games for common sizes.

Section 2: Set up registration (~300 words)
  What to collect (team name, coach contact, division/age group). Waitlist vs. 
  hard cap. What information you need before building the schedule.
  → Natural product moment: "If you're using FieldLogicHQ, open registration 
    in under 10 minutes — [link to signup]."

Section 3: Build the schedule (~400 words)
  Assigning games across fields and time slots. The math for a standard 
  round-robin. Common mistakes (uneven rest, venue conflicts, odd team counts).
  → Natural product moment: "FieldLogicHQ's schedule generator handles 
    this — field assignments, time slots, and conflict checking automatically."

Section 4: Publish and communicate (checklist, ~200 words)
  What coaches need before game day. How to share the bracket. 
  A short checklist: schedule published, registration closed, teams notified, 
  scoring ready.

Section 5: Run the day (~200 words)
  Score entry, live standings updates, what to do for a cancellation. Short.

Section 6: Archive and wrap up (~150 words)
  Final standings, why preserving history matters (next year's seeding, 
  team memory). One paragraph.

CTA (bottom of page):
  "Run your round-robin on FieldLogicHQ — free, no credit card required.
   Registration to final standings in one place. [Start your organization →]"
```

**What makes this work:**
- The guide answers the Google query completely — someone could run a tournament using only this article, without FieldLogicHQ
- The product appears at the "this is where you'd want a tool" moments — not as a pitch up front
- The CTA is earned: the reader has just been walked through the full workflow and knows the platform does all of it
- No dates, no author bios — permanent reference content that doesn't age

---

### Principles

**Content strategy for a small team must be:**
- **SEO-first** — every piece creates passive inbound. No content that doesn't rank for a specific query.
- **Audience-specific** — write for a tournament director, not "sports managers in general." Specificity earns trust.
- **Durable** — how-to content about running a round-robin is as relevant in three years as today. No news, no trends, no timeliness required.
- **One audience per piece** — write for the specific persona who will search the specific query that brings them here.

### Facebook Community Strategy

Volunteer org administrators congregate in:
- Sport-specific Facebook groups (e.g., "Ontario Minor Hockey Organizers", regional soccer groups, etc.)
- Provincial sport association community pages
- Local youth sports league parent and admin groups

**The approach:**  
This is not broadcast advertising. It's authentic participation in communities where the audience already has conversations.

**What works:**
- Answering specific questions about scheduling, registration, or tournament management (without pitching; just being useful)
- Sharing a how-to article when it's genuinely relevant to a discussion happening in the group
- Introducing the platform when someone explicitly asks "what does everyone use for tournaments?"

**What doesn't work (and damages trust):**
- Posting promotional content in groups unprompted
- Joining groups just to drop links
- Responding to every conversation with a product mention

**The rule:** Add value first, every time. The platform introduction happens naturally, or not at all. One genuine helpful comment from a knowledgeable person is worth more than twenty broadcast posts.
